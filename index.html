Josephine Baker

Activist | French Resistance Agent | Entertainer

Main life events

  • 1906 – Freda Josephine Baker (née MacDonald) born in St Louis, Missouri
  • 1925 – Sails to Paris, France where she gets her first big break. She becomes an overnight sensation, becoming a European icon for the roaring twenties
  • 1937 – Becomes a French national and renounces her U.S. citizenship
  • 1939 to 1945 – Becomes a French Resistance agent in World War 2. As no one suspected this great entertainer to be involved in the war, she was able to get information at parties and events she attended. She would then pass this information along with invisible ink on her sheet music
  • 1950s onward – Contributed to the civil rights movement. She was offered a leadership position in the movement following the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr. She turned this down out of concern for her children
  • 1975 – Passes away in Paris, France

Factoids

  • She received various honours and awards for her work in WW2: the Resistance Medal, Croix de Guerre, named Chevalier of the Légion d’honneur
  • She is the first black woman to star in a major motion picture. She is also the first black woman to be interred into to Panthéon (in 2021)
  • She would refuse to perform for segregated audiences and was disappointed in the racist way she was treated every time she went back to the U.S.
  • In Paris, she rubbed shoulders with the likes of Pablo Picasso, Ernest Hemmingway, and Ada “Bricktop” Smith
  • In her work as a civil rights activist, she had contact with Martin Luther King Jr, Rosa Parks, and Daisy Bates
  • She had four ex-husbands before her partner Robert Brady, including second husband William Baker (who’s surname she kept), and industrialist Jean Lion
  • Unable to have children of her own, she adopted 12 children over the course of her lifetime
